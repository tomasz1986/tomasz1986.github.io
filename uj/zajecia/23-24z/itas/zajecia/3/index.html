1. What is Korean language?

  • native language of 80 million people
    • South Korea: 52 million
    • North Korea: 26 million
    • Korean diaspora: 2 million
  • official and national language of South and North Korea
  • minority language in many parts of the world
    • China (Chaoxianzu, 조선족)
    • post-Soviet states (Koryo-saram, 고려인)
    • United States (jaemi gyopo, 재미 교포)
    • Japan (jaeil gyopo, 재일 교포)
  • written in Korean alphabet
    • South Korea: Hangeul (한글)
    • North Korea: Chosŏn'gŭl (조선글)
  • growing interest in Korean language
    • part of Korean Wave (한류, Hallyu)
    • military and diplomatic alliances (ROK-US, DPRK-PRC)

2. History of Korean language

  • Old Korean (고대 한국어)
    • Proto–Three Kingdoms period (2nd century BCE - 3rd century)
    • Three Kingdoms period (3rd century - 668)
    • Unified Silla period (668 - 935)
    • first historically documented stage of Korean language
    • based on the language of Silla kingdom
    • written in Chinese characters adapted to Korean syntax
    • introduction of Sino-Korean vocabulary
    • Sino-Korean pronunciation based on Old Chinese pronunciation
    • few surviving representations
      • vernacular poems (hyangga, 향가)
      • inscriptions
      • wooden slips (mokgan, 목간)
      • Buddhist works
  • Middle Korean (중세 한국어)
    • Goryeo period (936 - 1392)
    • Joseon period (1392 - 16th century)
    • divided into Early and Late periods
    • introcution of the Korean alphabet (1446)
    • written in Chinese characters in the Early period
    • started using the Korean alphabet in the Late period
    • introduction of new vocabulary during the Mongol invasions (13th century)
  • Early Modern Korean (근세 한국어)
    • late Joseon dynasty (17th century - 19th century)
    • formal writing until late 19th century written in Classical Chinese
  • Modern Korean (근현대 한국어)
    • 20th century and later
    • heavily influenced by Japanese during the occupation (late 19th century - 1945)
    • treated as a second class language after the Japanese language
    • Korean people forced to learn and speak Japanese in official situations
    • introduced new vocabulary to describe Western concepts
    • divided into North and South after the liberation
    • abolishment of Chinese characters in the North, and steady decline in the South
    • introduced loan words from European languages, e.g. Portuguese, German, Russian, English
