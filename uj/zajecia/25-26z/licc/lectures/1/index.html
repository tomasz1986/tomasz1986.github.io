Great Confucian scholars

Confucius (Kongzi, 孔子, 공자)

  • 551–479 BC (late Zhou dynasty)
  • founder of Confucianism

Mencius (Mengzi, 孟子, 맹자)

  • 371–289 BC (late Zhou dynasty)
  • second great Confucian philosopher
  • proponent of “good human nature”

Xunzi (荀子, 순자)

  • 310 – 238 BC (late Zhou dynasty)
  • third great Confucian philosopher
  • proponent of "evil human nature"

Zhu Xi (朱熹, 주자)

  • 1130 – 1200 AD (Southern Song dynasty)
  • father of Neo-Confucianism
  • reinterpretation of Confucian tradition

Four Books and Five Classics (四書五經, 사서오경)

  • 전근대 동아시아 지식인들이 학습했다는 사서오경의 난이도
  • 조선시대 선비들의 필독서 사서삼경 四書三經

Four Books (Sishu, 四書, 오경)

  • Chinese classics selected by Zhu Xi
  • general introduction to Confucianism
  • foundation of Confucian values and belief systems
  • core of official curriculum for civil service examinations
  1. Great Learning (Daxue, 大學, 대학)
  2. Doctrine of the Mean (Zhongyong, 中庸, 중용)
  3. Analects (Lunyu, 論語, 논어)
  4. Mencius (Mengzi, 孟子, 맹자)

Five Classics (Wujing, 五經, 오경)

  • pre-Qin dynasty (221–206 BC) Chinese books
  • part of official Confucian curriculum since Han dynasty (202 BC–9 AD; 25–220 AD)
  1. Classic of Poetry (Shijing, 詩經, 시경)
  2. Book of Documents (Shujing, 書經, 서경)
  3. Book of Rites (Liji, 禮記, 예기)
  4. Book of Changes (Yijing, 易經, 역경)
  5. Spring and Autumn Annals (Chunqiu, 春秋, 춘추)

Three Bonds (Sangang, 삼강) and Five Virtues (Wuchang, 오륜)

  • three most important human bonds
  • five most important virtues
  • moral and political rules of Confucianism
  • closely related to yin-yang philosophy (阴阳, 음양)

Three Bonds

  1. 군위신강 (君爲臣綱): Subjects must follow their ruler.
  2. 부위자강 (父爲子綱): Children must follow their father.
  3. 부위부강 (夫爲婦綱): Wife must follow her husband.

Five Virtues

  1. 부자유친 (父子有親): There must be friendship between father and children.
  2. 군신유의 (君臣有義): There must be justice between ruler and subjects.
  3. 부부유별 (夫婦有別): There must be difference between husband and wife.
  4. 장유유서 (長幼有序): There must be hierarchy between older and younger.
  5. 붕우유신 (朋友有信): There must be trust between friends.

Rise of Confucianism in China and East Asia

  • dawn of Zhou dynasty (1045–221 BC) as the golden age for Confucius
    • ruling through moral example
    • practicing time-honoured rituals
    • prevailing social harmony
  • split of China into small, independent, warring states around 700 BC
    • ruled by many small, feudal lords
    • authority maintained through laws, punishment, and force
    • lack of moral behaviour and concern for the people's welfare
  • Confucius's goal to restore the moral order of the early Zhou
    • good government
    • proper social relations
    • respectful treatment of others
    • correct ritual performance
  • lack of interest in Confucianism by the feudal warlords
  • change of Confucius's focus from rulers to his disciples
    • teaching disciples instead of engaging in politics
    • hope for embracing his political ideas and future succession in official life
    • compilation of the Analects as the main source of his thought
  • Five Classics regarded as canonical in the Confucian tradition
    • principles of sage government
    • descriptions of proper ritual practice
    • beliefs about organisation of the universe
    • relationship between the universe and humankind
    • lessons from the Spring and Autumn period (771–256 BC)
  • general concerns of the “hundred schools of thought”
    • What makes for an effective ruler?
    • What makes for an effective government?
    • What is the ideal relationship between the government and the people?
    • How can China achieve the unity, stability, and prosperity it once knew?
    • What are the responsibilities of the individual to his family, community, and state?
    • What is man's place in the universe?
  • competition between Confucianism and other teachings
    • lively and prominent representatives (e.g. Mencius, Xunzi)
    • not yet favoured over teachings of other thinkers
  • development of Confucianism under Han dynasty (202 BC–222 AD)
    • privileged status at the court provided by Emperor Wu (141–87 BC)
    • promotion of Confucian teachings and dismissal of non-Confucians from offices
    • creation of Imperial Academy in 124 BC to teach Confucian ideas
    • appointment to the official bureaucracy based on examinations
  • Confucianism becoming the state ideology of China
    • essential ideological foundation of the imperial Chinese state
    • reliance on Confucian teachings for guidance and legitimacy
    • recruitment of government officials based on Confucian examinations
    • education in China centred around mastery of Confucian writings
  • Spread of Confucian ideology to other Asian countries
    • establishment of a Confucian academy in Goguryeo in 372 AD
    • establishment of a Confucian academy in Silla in 682 AD
    • introduction of Confucian examination system in Silla in 788 AD
    • expansion of the examination system in the Goryeo period (918–1392)
    • peak of Confucian influence during the Joseon dynasty (1392–1910)
    • transmission of Chinese texts through Korea to Japan since 5th century AD
    • long-time prevalence of Buddhism and Shintō over Confucianism in Japan
    • raise of Confucianism's popularity in the Tokugawa period (1603–1868)
    • Confucian teachings as the moral basis of Japanese rulers and society
    • spread of Confucianism to Vietnam under Chinese rule from 111 BC to 939 AD
    • adoption of Chinese culture (writing, ideology, administration) in Vietnam
    • Confucian examinations as the basis of Vietnam's court officials recruitment until 1910s
