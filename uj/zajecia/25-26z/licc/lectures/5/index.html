Legacy and Influence of Confucian Culture on Modern Korean Society

Greeting and Etiquette

1. Hierarchy in the Korean Culture

  • deep-rooted hierarchy as one of characteristics of Korean society
  • applies to both private and professional life
  • found in all spheres of life, e.g. language, home, school, work
  • consciously or subconsciously followed by Koreans in their life

1.1 High Power Distance and Low Individualism

  • power distance = distance between less powerful person and more powerful person in social system
  • all social classes understand and mostly accept social inequalities
  • higher classes on top of social ladder with belief that they are better than others
  • lower people (아랫사람, araessaram) expected to respect higher people (윗사람, wissaram)
  • social position determined on placement within social group (family, friends, work, etc.)

1.2 Confucianism and Its Impact on the Social Structure

  • Neo-Confucianism as state ideology in Joseon dynasty (1392–1910)
  • combination of religion, philosophy, and education
  • collectivism more important than individual rights or desires
  • determines individual's whole life, from birth to death
  • one of foundations of traditional social system in Korea
  • still has tremendous influence on modern Korean society
  • Three Bonds
    1. 군위신강 (君爲臣綱, gunwisingang): Subjects must follow their ruler.
    2. 부위자강 (父爲子綱, buwijagang): Children must follow their father.
    3. 부위부강 (夫爲婦綱, buwibugang): Wife must follow her husband.
  • Five Virtues
    1. 부자유친 (父子有親, bujayuchin): There must be friendship between father and children.
    2. 군신유의 (君臣有義, gunsinyueui): There must be justice between ruler and subjects.
    3. 부부유별 (夫婦有別, bubuyubyeol): There must be difference between husband and wife.
    4. 장유유서 (長幼有序, jangyuyuseo): There must be hierarchy between older and younger.
    5. 붕우유신 (朋友有信, bunguyusin): There must be trust between friends.

2. Family as the Source of Interpersonal Relations

  • family and not individual as main social unit
  • traditionally determined individual's life, e.g. education, work, marriage, etc.
  • lost some importance in 20thcentury
    • Japanese occupation (1910–1945)
    • Korean War (1950–1953)
    • economic modernisation (after 1960s)
    • Westernisation
  • still one of most important values in life of Korean people
  • willing to sacrifice individual freedom for family

2.1 Family Etiquette

  1. Language etiquette
    • use polite language (높임말) to parents and elders
    • listen carefully when parents and elders are speaking
    • answer politely when called by elders
    • wait to speak until elders have finished their speech
    • say “thank you” (고맙습니다) after receiving help from elders
    • say “it was my fault” (잘못했습니다) and “I am sorry” (죄송합니다) to elders after doing wrong or making a mistake
  2. Greeting etiquette
    • say „good night” (안녕히 주무세요) before going to bed
    • say „hello” (잘 다녀왔습니다) and „good bye” (잘 다녀오겠습니다) when leaving or coming back
    • greet parents when they come back to the house (안녕히 다녀오셨어요?)
    • say „I enjoyed the meal” (잘 먹었습니다) after dining together
  3. Dining etiquette
    • wait for all family to gather and only start eating after elders
    • keep proper posture when sitting at the table
    • do not make noises when chewing food or using dishes
    • speak in quiet voice, only after swallowing the food
    • after the meal, put away your dishes in the sink

3. The Importance of Bowing in the Life of Koreans

  • omnipresent bowing when meeting people in Korea
  • integral part of Korean culture and language
  • complex usage of titles and language formalities
  • using titles or names followed by titles in professional relations
  • strict hierarchy and complex rules of greetings
  • different bows depending on position, social status, age, etc.

3.1 Meaning of greeting (인사, insa)

  • insa from Chinese characters 人事 means both “greeting” and “human relations”
  • bowing as the main form of greeting between people in Korea
  • different bow "depth" depending on situation and personal relation
  • requires prior knowledge on when to use which type of greeting
  • taught to children in educational institutions from the very young age
  • learning resources provided by the Korean government for new workers
  • more freedom in private relations than in professional relations

3.2 Types of Greeting in Korean Culture

No Type Posture Usage
1 목례 (mongnye, head nod) 5° bow thanking for treats/snacks, thanking for help with carrying luggage, meeting the same person often
2 약례 (yangnye, light bow) 15° bow basic greeting in narrow spaces, greeting one's superior for the second time
3 보통례 (botongnye, normal bow) 30° bow everyday greeting used for acquaintances, co-workers, superiors
4 정중례 (jeongjungnye, polite bow) 45° bow welcoming customers in a shop, thanking or apologising for something
5 가장 정중례 (gajang jeongjungnye, very polite bow) 90° bow expressing deep gratitude or respect
6 큰절 (keunjeol, great bow) bowing on one's knees during wedding or funeral ceremonies
7 others shaking hands, putting arms around, hugging

4.1 Greeting as the Foundation of Etiquette

  • proper greeting should be learnt and practiced by new employees
  • employees can be judged by the way of their greeting
  • the basis for etiquette and starting point for human relations
  • greeting shows devotion to the company, respect for superiors, affection between coworkers, and proper attitude towards customers
  • importance of greeting:
    • shows respect for others
    • shows friendliness and cooperation
    • focuses on giving instead of receiving
    • maintains social life and friendly relations

4.2 How to Greet

  • all greetings should be polite and natural
  • one should smile brightly, dress neatly, and look happy
  • greeting should be done at the right moment
    • in 30 feet distance when walking towards each other
    • in 6 feet when going in the same direction
    • immediately when meeting unexpectedly
    • in place and slowly when meeting superiors with guests
  • one should keep proper posture together with lowering one's head
  • greeting should be done firmly and with confidence

4.4 Situational Greetings

  • when coming to work
    • greet others politely when entering the office
    • stand up before greeting your superiors when they enter the office
    • the standard form of greeting by lowering head by 30 degrees is enough
    • when late, approach your superior, then apologise and explain the reason without providing excuses
  • when leaving work
    • tell coworkers that you are leaving (먼저 퇴근하겠습니다; 먼저 가보겠습니다)
    • do not use expressions like 수고하십시오, 수고하셨습니다, etc. to your superiors
    • say „goodbye” (안녕히 가십시오; 내일 뵙겠습니다) to coworkers who are leaving
    • if your superior is staying at the office, ask them if there is any more work to do
  • meeting more than once
    • the first greeting should be respectful and cheerful
    • the next greetings should be just a head nod with a bright smile
  • when being busy working
    • should be just a head nod if the situation allows it
    • need not be done if the work requires complete concentration
  • when meeting a new person
    • use a standard way of greeting when meeting for the first time
    • find out their position and have a small talk when meeting next time
  • running into someone inside the bathroom
    • should not go out of the way to greet them
    • only nod your head if you have eye contact
  • when meeting customers
    • greet them with “welcome” (어서 오십시오)
    • greet them politely, lowering your head by 45 degrees
    • if sitting, you must stand up before greeting them
    • when they leave, use the same polite way of greeting
