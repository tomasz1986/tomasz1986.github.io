Legacy and Influence of Confucian Culture on Modern Korean Society

Korean Family

1. Concept of Uri (우리)

  • individualism vs collective spirit
    • selfhood shaped and defined by the community
    • defining relationships based on the common group
    • group-specific transformation by individual members
    • definition of “self” via relationships and group membership
    • importance of social harmony, relations with others, and social expectations
    • individual privacy and needs less important than those of a group
    • individuals feeling responsibility for the whole group
    • regarding “Korea” (우리나라) as one extended family
  • becoming “one” instead of staying “individual”
    • belief that people are interconnected
    • fear of separation from other people
    • lack of distinction between “I” and “we” (e.g. “우리 집/학교”, “우리 엄마/아빠/아내/남편”)
    • inability of equal division (e.g. when paying for food in a restaurant)
    • hesitance to part from one another (e.g. after having a meeting)
    • dependence of children on their parents (e.g. living and sleeping in the same room, solving problems for children)
  • avoiding confrontation with other people
    • stress and pressure in modern competitive society
    • forming groups to deal with stress together (e.g. 동창회, 동향회, 산악회)
    • relying on relations based on blood, school, region, etc.
    • relieving stress on one hand, creating “group egoism” on other hand
  • nationalism and xenophobia

2. Concept of “Jeong” (정, 情)

  • closely related to “우리” (like an umbrella embracing everyone in terms of “uri”)
  • symbol of motherly love and care in a familial environment
  • emotional and psychological bonds joining people in a Korean cultural context
  • the basis of dividing world into different degrees of “us” (우리) and “them” (남)
  • sharing feelings through solidarity and compassion inside a community
  • extension beyond biological ties (family) to strangers (friends, places, etc.)
  • tying people together in the category of “uri” (even if they are not family)
  • formed through relationships, relationships consolidated via “jeong” (정이 쌓이다)
  • extension beyond family and friends to places (e.g. school, company) or objects (e.g. phone, car)
  • usually expressed with unspoken actions (e.g. sharing food, helping each other, doing activities together)
  • habitual and unreflective attachment to relationships even in unwanted situations
  • endless Korean expressions using “정”:
    • 정을 나누다
    • 정을 느끼다
    • 정을 떼다
    • 정을 붙이다
    • 정을 쏟다
    • 정이 가다
    • 정이 그립다
    • 정이 두텁다/깊다
    • 정이 들다
    • 정이 떨어지다
    • 정이 많다
    • 정이 밉다
    • 정 때문에
    • 정을 통하다

3. Concept of Family (가족, 家族)

  • ideal traditional family = husband & wife, first son & wife, first grandson & wife
  • the smallest unit forming Korean society and state
  • the only group of people who become “one” completely
  • the natural basis for all moral and political behavior
  • the most biologically rooted human institution
  • providing home for individuals who cannot live alone
  • impossible to take apart (e.g. people after divorce come back to their families)
  • weakening of Korean family during Japanese occupation (e.g. abolition of sadang / family shrines)
  • foundation of family grounded in filial piety and love between siblings
  • Confucian belief that strong families lead to a strong state
  • relationship between parents and children as important as relationship between ruler and subjects
  • impossible to imagine living outside of a family
  • “If the family lives in harmony, all affairs will prosper” (가화만사성, 家和萬事成)

4. Family-centric Society

  • concept of filial piety (효, 孝)
    • sacrificing one's individuality for parents
    • supporting parents financially (e.g. “filial tourism”)
    • tying one's future with parental care (e.g. becoming a doctor to take care of sick parents)
  • endless parental love for children, and older siblings for younger siblings
    • sacrificing their own lives for children
    • finding sense of life in their children
    • taking pleasure in leaving large inheritance for children
    • working hard to help younger siblings
  • belief that the state relies on a strong family, and a weak family means a weak state
  • difficult social position of people with no family
    • loneliness leading to drastic consequences (e.g. suicide)
  • lack of will to become independent by children from parents, and by parents from children
  • marriage as union of two families
    • asking parents for permission when choosing one's partner
    • interference of parents into married couple's life
    • conflicts between spouses developing into conflicts between two families
    • arranged marriages to match people from similar backgrounds
  • preference of family over other social groups (e.g. lack of will to donate money)

5. Roles of Men and Women

  • patriarchy since Joseon dynasty (1392–1897) grounded in Neo-Confucianism
  • public domain for men vs private space for women
    • 아내 (안+애), 안사람, 집사람, 바깥사람, 바깥양반
    • female members as “good daughters/wives/mothers”
    • male members as heads of households
    • male roles as leaders in society and politics
  • concept of namjonyeobi (남존여비, 男尊女卑):
    • belief that men are superior and women are inferior
    • before marriage, a women should obey her father
    • after marriage, a women should obey her husband
    • after the husband's death, a women should obey her son
    • Instructions for women (내훈, 內訓) by Queen Sohye (소혜왕후) written in 1522:

      According to Confucius, a wife should be submissive to her husband, not handle affairs by herself, and fulfill ‘three obediences (samjong 三從)’: Obey her father when unmarried, obey her husband when married, and obey her son after her husband dies. This means that a woman should never handle things alone.

      The husband is the heaven of his wife. The wife should respect him as if she serves her father…. If your husband is angry, you must not say words until he calms down. Even if he beats you, how dare you bear a grudge? Men are superior to women while women are inferior to men (namjonyeobi 男尊女卑). It is absolutely fair given the relationship, if your husband hits and scolds you. How could you talk back and show your anger?

  • predominant and powerful role of women inside the house
  • concept of sallim (살림):
    • taking responsibility of household and care of everyday tasks
    • inseparable connection between sallim and jeong
    • household tasks traditionally always assigned to women (mothers)
    • nurturing, feeding, and educating essential both for the family and society
    • unpaid and unrecognised care work inside the household
  • idealised womenhood as advisers, educators, and caregivers (현모양처, 賢母良妻, wise mother and good wife)
  • expectations for women to develop domestic skills required to run a house (e.g. cooking, sewing)
  • expectations for women to develop moral and intellectual skills required to raise children
  • being a wise mother and good wife a barometer to judge woman as qualified or relatively worthless
  • rejecting the traditional model of womanhood considered selfish, unwomanly, unruly, disobedient, etc.
  • women expected to comply with male authorities and hide their real feelings and opinions
  • large number of proverbs in Korean langage criticising women who do not fit the expected role:
    • 암탉이 울면 집안이 망한다.
    • 여자 목소리가 담장을 넘으면 안된다.
    • 여자 셋이 모이면 접시가 깨진다.
  • both men and women expected to follow stereotypical standards:
    • 여자가 칠칠치 못하게
    • 남자는 우는 것이 아니다.
    • 남자가 그것도 못해?
    • 남자/여자다워야 한다.
    • 김여사/된장녀/김치녀/김치남/퐁퐁남
  • strict separation and different roles of men and women during traditional holidays
    • preparation for jesa ceremony as women's responsibility
    • performing jesa ceremony as male's responsibility
