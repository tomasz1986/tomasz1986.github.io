1. Personal Honorifics in Korean Language

  • formal (gyeoksikche)
    • polite (nopimmal)
      • very polite (ajunopim): hasipsioche, e.g. gamnida, gamnikka, gasipsio
      • moderately polite (yesanopim): haoche, e.g. gapsida, gasio
    • impolite (banmal)
      • moderately impolite (yesanatchum): hageche, e.g. gage
      • very impolite (ajunatchum): haerache, e.g. gara
  • informal (bigyeoksikche)
    • polite (nopimmal)
      • universally polite (durunopim): haeyoche, e.g. gayo
    • impolite (banmal)
      • universally impolite (durunatchum): haeche, e.g. ga

3.2 Observations

  • Korean language textbooks are heavily skewed towards teaching informal polite forms of nopimmal (haeyoche).
  • The vast majority of dialogues are between equal persons using the same level of politeness and formality (e.g. two friends).
  • There is a lack of dialogues between unequal persons that would use different levels of formality and politeness towards each other (e.g. teacher and student, older and younger person, adult and child, etc.).
  • Even when the relationship is unequal (e.g. teacher and student), both persons in most cases still use the same informal polite language forms.
  • There are zero dialogues using family titles (e.g. nuna, hyeong, eonni, oppa, ajeossi, ajumeoni, etc.) which are prevalent in real-life relationships and conversations between native Korean speakers.
  • Due to lack of exposure to other types of formality and politeness, foreign learners of Korean tend to rely on using the same informal polite forms of nopimmal (haeyoche) regardless of the situation and the person to whom they are speaking.
